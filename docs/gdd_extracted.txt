3. DESIGN CONCEPT
3.1.
Creative Concept
3.1.1.
Creative Objectives
The creative objective of this design is to create a digital game to communicate the importance of digital literacy in the online world for Generation Z, aged 18-22. Beyond serving as entertainment, this game acts as a reminder for players to remain vigilant during online interactions and to take steps to protect themselves.
3.1.2.
Creative Strategy
3.1.2.1. Topic and Theme 
The topic and theme of this design is a roleplay game where the player acts as the owner of a computer and mobile phone repair shop. By placing the player directly in contact with digital devices and their owners, the theme of digital literacy becomes more relevant within the gameplay. Furthermore, the game provides a challenge by using interaction between the player and characters to solve problems.
3.1.2.2. Target Audience
Demographic: Ages 18-22, male and female.
Geographic: Living in urban areas in Indonesia. (game is still in english dw)
Psychographic: Possess a sense of curiosity and an interest in digital media.
Behavior: Enjoy playing games, frequently use computers and mobile phones, and are active on social media.
Technographic: Capable of operating computers and mobile phones for communication and information retrieval.
3.1.2.3. Visual Concept 
This digital game design will utilize a 2D visual approach with a pixel art style. This style is closely tied to the history of computer and digital game development and remains relevant today. Additionally, this style provides a playful and simple impression.

3.1.3.
Primary Media
3.1.3.1.
Design Concept
This design uses digital game media on a Windows-based desktop platform. The core gameplay loop involves interacting with characters and solving puzzles to repair their devices. Through these interactions, players are encouraged to read client dialogues carefully to identify vulnerabilities caused by careless digital behavior. These vulnerabilities may include clicking links without verifying authenticity or downloading files from unknown sources.
3.1.3.2.
Design Title
The title for this game is Troubleshooter. The term "troubleshoot" refers to the act of finding the source of a problem and solving it, a term frequently used in the context of computers and technology.
3.1.3.3.
Game Contents
a. Menu Content
Start: Begin the game.
Settings: Game configurations.
Quit: Exit the game.
b. Core Gameplay
Diagnosis: Players must converse with device owners to understand the issue. Client dialogues serve as clues to unlock specific puzzle options. Players must determine the type of problem; choosing incorrectly results in a payment penalty.
Bloat: Unnecessary applications running simultaneously. Unlike malware, bloat does not necessarily aim to steal data or damage the machine.
Malware: Dangerous code or applications attached to the device.
System Failure: System errors usually caused by updates or user-made changes.
Puzzle Solving: After a correct diagnosis, players solve a themed puzzle: Cleanup for bloat, Scan and Remove for malware, and System Revert for system failure.
Payment: A points system (starting at a default of 100) that tracks performance. Points are deducted for errors. At the end of the in-game week, the total is displayed to the player.
Through these phases, players are introduced to vital digital security terms and learn the consequences of unsafe digital habits.
c. Rules
Each "day" lasts 3-4 minutes.
Mistakes during puzzles reduce payment.
If time or payment runs out, the day is failed, and no payment is received.
Successful completion awards the remaining payment as the final score for that day.
d. flowchart

Keyboard: Used for dialogue interactions with characters.
